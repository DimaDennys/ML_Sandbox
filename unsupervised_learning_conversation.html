Unsupervised learning is a type of machine learning where algorithms find patterns in data without labeled examples. Here's what you should know:

What It Is

Unlike supervised learning (where you train on input-output pairs), unsupervised learning lets the algorithm discover structure on its own. The data has no "right answers" to guide it.

Core Techniques

Clustering — grouping similar data points together

  • K-Means: divides data into K clusters by minimising distance to centroids
  • DBSCAN: finds clusters of arbitrary shape based on density
  • Hierarchical clustering: builds a tree of nested clusters

Dimensionality Reduction — compressing data while preserving structure

  • PCA (Principal Component Analysis): finds directions of maximum variance
  • t-SNE / UMAP: great for visualising high-dimensional data in 2D/3D
  • Autoencoders: neural networks that learn compressed representations

Density Estimation — learning the underlying data distribution

  • Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs): models data as a mix of Gaussian distributions
  • Kernel Density Estimation (KDE): non-parametric distribution estimation

Generative Models — learning to generate new data

  • VAEs (Variational Autoencoders): encode data into a latent space and decode it
  • GANs (Generative Adversarial Networks): two networks compete to produce realistic data

Key Use Cases

  • Customer segmentation
  • Anomaly/fraud detection
  • Recommendation systems
  • Topic modelling in text (e.g. LDA)
  • Image compression
  • Feature learning for downstream tasks

Key Challenges

  • No ground truth — hard to evaluate how well it's working
  • Choosing the right number of clusters (e.g., picking K in K-Means)
  • Interpretability — clusters or components may not have obvious meaning
  • Sensitivity to scale — many algorithms require feature normalisation

How to Evaluate It

Since there are no labels, you rely on:

  • Silhouette score — how well-separated clusters are
  • Inertia / Within-cluster sum of squares
  • Elbow method — for choosing K
  • Domain knowledge — do the results make practical sense?

The biggest takeaway: unsupervised learning is powerful for exploration and representation, especially when labelling data is expensive or impossible. It's often a first step before applying supervised methods.

Here is an overview of all four techniques with both general and industrial examples, followed by deep dives for each.

Overview of all four techniques with industrial and general examples

Clustering

Industrial: Predictive maintenance on a production line

Imagine a bottling plant with 50 sensors per machine — vibration, temperature, pressure, RPM — logging every second. No one has labelled which readings are "about to fail." You run K-Means on a rolling 10-minute window of sensor data. The algorithm discovers 4 natural groups: normal operation, warm-up state, thermal stress, and a small fourth cluster that precedes 80% of past breakdowns. Now the line stops before the failure, not after.

General: E-commerce customer segmentation

An online retailer with 2 million customers uses K-Means on purchase frequency, average order value, and product categories. The algorithm discovers 5 segments: bargain hunters, loyal high-spenders, seasonal buyers, browse-only, and bulk B2B buyers. Each gets a tailored campaign — nobody decided these groups in advance, the data revealed them.

Dimensionality Reduction

Industrial: Real-time motor health monitoring (wind turbines)

A wind turbine farm logs 300+ signals per turbine (vibration on 3 axes, bearing temperatures, gearbox acoustics, current harmonics). PCA compresses these into 3 principal components that capture 94% of variance. Engineers plot every turbine as a dot in a 2D space. Turbines drifting away from the main cluster are flagged for inspection — no threshold rules, no hand-crafted features required.

General: Genomics research visualisation

A cancer research lab has RNA sequencing data: 20,000 gene expression values per patient cell sample. UMAP reduces this to 2 dimensions. The result reveals 7 cell subtypes — including a rare one that only appears in patients who didn't respond to treatment. This single visualisation redirects years of lab work.

Density Estimation

Industrial: Anomaly detection in hydraulic press systems

A Gaussian Mixture Model is trained on months of "normal" operating data from a hydraulic press. The GMM learns that normal operation follows 3 Gaussian distributions (light load, medium load, heavy load). Any reading with log-likelihood below a threshold is flagged — catching a slow oil leak 3 weeks before catastrophic failure, something a simple threshold alarm would have missed.

General: Fraud detection in banking

A bank trains a GMM on millions of legitimate transactions. When a transaction arrives, its likelihood under the learned distribution is scored. A £4,000 charge at 3am from an overseas merchant codes as extremely low-density — flagged instantly. Unlike rule-based systems, density estimation catches novel fraud patterns that don't match any known rule.

Generative Models

Industrial: Synthetic fault data for rare failure modes

A turbine manufacturer wants to train a supervised fault classifier but has only 12 real examples of a rare bearing failure (out of 50,000 normal cycles). A VAE is trained on those 12 examples, learns the latent distribution, then generates 5,000 synthetic fault cycles. The resulting classifier achieves 91% recall on that rare class, up from 34% without synthetic augmentation.

General: Synthetic medical data for privacy-safe research

A hospital can't share real patient records due to GDPR. A GAN is trained on de-identified data and learns to produce realistic synthetic patient records that match the statistical properties of the real dataset but correspond to no actual person. Researchers can now build and validate models on 500,000 synthetic patients — with zero privacy risk.
